> What software did you use to create your data visualization?

I used Tableau for this visualization.

> Who is your intended audience? 
    
My intended audiences are diners at restaurants/takeout places, government food safety regulators, and restaurant/franchise owners.

> What information or message are you trying to convey with your visualization? 

I am trying to convey the total number of food safety infractions that have occurred per year with food establishements in Toronto. I wanted to show how many of the infractions were significant/crucial infractions, which are the more serious types of infractions, and how that compared with the total number of infractions.
    
> What design principles (substantive, perceptual, aesthetic) did you consider when making your visualization? How did you apply these principles? With what elements of your plots? 

I wanted to keep a simple and minimalistic design, while also making it aesthetically pleasing. I wanted to make sure that the data was honestly and accurately presented without any bias. I wanted to make sure that the message of what I was trying to show was clear. This meant making the title accurately reflect what was being shown in the visualization, while providing some clarity through minimal text. I wanted to make it clear that some years (2022 and 2025) were missing data from certain months, which is why I decided to include some textboxes above the bars for those specific years. I also wanted to explain the importance of the significant/crucial infractions, which is why I also included a small textbox for that. 

I made sure the colours I used were accessible, and that the titles, legend, and font were easy to read. I also made sure to state the source of the data clearly at the bottom. 
    
> How did you ensure that your data visualizations are reproducible? If the tool you used to make your data visualization is not reproducible, how will this impact your data visualization? 

This data visualization is not as reproducible as my first visualization as this was not done in Python. Since I used Tableau, it would mean someone who wanted to reproduce it would have to know how to use the software. However, as it is on Tableau Public, the file can be downloaded so if someone really wanted to reproduce it, they just have to know which dataset was used and also have knowledge on the program itself. It won't have any major impact on my data visualization as it can still be reproduced, but just not as easily as it requires the use of Tableau. 
    
> How did you ensure that your data visualization is accessible?  

I made sure that the colours used were accessible. I also made sure the title, legend, and font size were easily readable. I made sure to keep the background clean and free of clutter so that the background colour would not clash with the colours of the bars. I also wanted to make sure that the text I did have was clearly distinguished by a border, and that it did not overlap any important visuals. 
    
> Who are the individuals and communities who might be impacted by your visualization?  

Individuals who dine out often may be alarmed at how many total infractions, including many serious infractions, there are in any given year. This may cause them to be more cautious on choosing a place to eat outside, or may cause them to reduce dining out as often. It may also impact business owners in the food industry as it may cause them to invest more time and resources to prevent getting any infractions, since they can see that the number is not low. This may also impact government food safety regulators as they can deem the number of infractions they see to be high, including the significant/crucial severity levels of these infractions, which may encourage increasing the number of inspections per year, or perhaps invest in food safety education for these businesses.
    
> How did you choose which features of your chosen dataset to include or exclude from your visualization? 

I chose the features that were relevant for what I wanted to show, which were year, infraction details (for the count), and severity in order to categorize significant/crucial infractions. The ones I did not need, I also excluded them before loading the dataset into Tableau so that it would upload quicker. 
    
> What ‘underwater labour’ contributed to your final data visualization product?

There was time taken to decide what type of information I wanted to convey, and what type of visualization to use. It was also very different from creating a visualization through Python, so it took time to play around on Tableau to create it in the way I was happy with. There was also a bit of time figuring out the exact reason why the year 2022 did not match the numbers of 2023 and onwards, and thinking of how to convey that information in a quick and minimal way that made sense.
